Active milestone
REJECTED END TO END · EXACT PATH-SCHUR3 Jul

A 1.53× exact contact-phase win does not improve real training

The experiment built the exact contact-space Schur operator from contacted body paths, solved compact PGS, and applied the summed body wrench through one deterministic depth-parallel ABA. It was physically clean, graph-captured, and substantially faster on the isolated all-active G1 contact state—but realistic activation patterns and extra phases erase the gain.

  • The isolated 8,192-world contact phase is 370.8 versus 567.8 µs (1.53×), with Schur error 2.4e−7, impulse error 3.0e−8, and generalized-delta error 8.9e−8.
  • The initial short G1 bracket suggested +3.1%, but the confirmation bracket measures about 915k/s path-Schur versus 918k/s reference: neutral/slightly slower. The retained 923k/s headline is unchanged.
  • Saturated 8,192-world ANYmal rejects the route at 1.57M versus 1.67M env-steps/s (−6.0%). The dense 34-point G1 scene also remains 1.3% slower after exact dimension-based fallback.
  • At 512 worlds, hardware-saturation fallback recovers reference performance: ANYmal 373.9k versus 373.5k/s and H1 352.7k versus 357.1k/s (the latter within short-bracket variance). Single/few-articulation scenes likewise retain the reference route.
  • Graph conditionals were tested and removed: their launch/control overhead made ANYmal and H1 slower, confirming they are not a free solution for sparse activation.
  • CUDA-graph tests pass for an active compact page, a 72-contact dense fallback, full state/contact parity within 2e−5, and spatial momentum conservation.
  • The complete experiment is preserved at 67f286e6 on twidmer/experiment-phoenx-path-schur. It is not merged into the retained solver.
  • A follow-up split ran the large fused contact/advance/publish kernel only for live-contact articulations and the lean advance/publish kernel for the inactive complement. Arbitrary-page graph parity passes, but matched G1 training falls about 845k → 822k/s (−2.7%): the extra complementary launch costs more than inactive early returns save.
  • The split is preserved at 51b834f4 on twidmer/experiment-phoenx-active-fused-split, not merged.
  • Conclusion: the current 923k/s solver is at a local plateau. More gains remain possible, but the next architecture must eliminate a phase without replacing it with another fleet-wide launch.

Static topology caching wins across G1, ANYmal, and H1

The post-cache Nsight result showed that row threads still repeatedly chased static parent, path, and DOF metadata. A 768-byte shared cache now stores local parent and DOF metadata once per articulation block; every exact row uses a bounded parent walk while the proven factor cache, row order, and articulated equations remain unchanged.

  • Commit 7bc6b868 is clean on twidmer/experiment-phoenx-staged-contact-rows. This is a general retained-candidate improvement, not yet the requested needle-moving row-free persistent architecture.
MEASURED · BUILDER WIN REJECTED END TO END3 Jul

Faster row construction alone does not move training throughput

The requested post-cache Nsight Compute capture and two exact follow-up prototypes now isolate the remaining problem. The retained headline stays at 923k samples/s; no higher number is being claimed.

  • After the retained shared-factor cache, contact-row construction falls 943.6 → 879.7 µs (−6.8%), but remains latency-bound: about 68% long-scoreboard issue spacing, only 19.8% scheduler cycles with an eligible warp, and 79% excessive global sectors.
  • An exact compressed 12D body-mobility cache improves its isolated response calculation by 6.7%, but only about 486–503 of 8192 training worlds are eligible. Build and launch overhead lowers complete training throughput, so the path is rejected.
  • An exact ≤4-contact shared-row builder reproduces Jacobians and articulated responses bit-for-bit and improves stable isolated builder medians about 8.5% (222 → 203 µs).
  • Integrated training nevertheless reaches 892.4k/s versus an immediate clean retained control at 922.8k/s: a real 3.3% regression. The extra launch and global row publication erase the local saving; this implementation is rejected.
  • Direct shared-memory PGS variants were also exact, but register/instruction pressure made them 5–40% slower than the proven tile solve.
  • Compiler-supported noinline phase bodies also fail: the exact by-value call ABI reaches about 793k/s, while passing aggregate descriptors by reference falls to about 509k/s because array/member accesses stay behind pointer loads. Both forms are rejected.
  • An exact contact-space Delassus solve and combined articulated impulse application pass graph parity, but reach only 892k/s after depth parallelization; they reduce storage without reducing the dominant per-row articulated work.
  • The next credible large experiment must remove row publication and phase boundaries while also reducing—not repackaging—the O(contact rows × articulation size) response construction.
RETAINED · CROSS-SCENE ROW CACHE3 Jul

Shared articulation factors remove the row builder's worst latency

The existing contact-row Nsight report showed a 943.6 µs kernel with 76.7% of issue spacing in long-scoreboard stalls and 73% excessive global sectors. Every one of 96 row threads repeatedly loaded the same motion subspaces, articulated-inertia columns, and inverse joint factors.

  • The row block now cooperatively loads those exact factors into about 4.6 KB of shared memory once, then evaluates the unchanged articulated response equations and deterministic row order.
  • Matched G1 graph-leapfrog training improves 913.8k → 923.0k samples/s (+1.0%). The retained nanoG1 gap is now about 1.38×.
  • Cross-scene gates also improve: 512-world ANYmal +0.5%, H1 +1.7%; 64 articulations in one world ANYmal +1.5%, H1 +1.7%. Final state magnitudes are identical.
  • Six focused CUDA regressions pass: exact generalized rows, arbitrary contact pages, persistent/reference parity, multiple grounded articulations per world, deterministic dense contacts, and spatial momentum conservation.
  • Commit 2ce2656a is retained on twidmer/experiment-phoenx-persistent-articulations. Repository-wide pre-commit remains blocked only by unrelated pre-existing lint/typo failures; focused lint and formatting pass.
  • Next: target the measured publication/dependency chain with a compact native/noinline persistent phase design; no further user profiling action is currently required.
Next decisions

Productionize the path-Schur survivor

The new candidate removes the dominant full-articulation response construction while retaining the exact Delassus operator and deterministic impulse application. Unlike earlier response-basis and contact-space attempts, its isolated win survives the complete build/solve/apply phase.

Next: integrate behind an explicit experimental path, measure full graph-leapfrog training early, and reject or specialize only from topology-derived runtime facts—not robot identities.

Do not repeat

  • Separate 32-row resident Jacobian/response solve: bit-exact but −8.6% G1 training from a second launch, fixed shared loads, and occupancy.
  • One-pass ABA / pre-contact-only Coriolis: +6% physics but severe momentum drift.
  • Compile-time velocity-only publish kernel: exact but +0.1% (neutral).
  • Factor/contact multi-stream overlap: −2.7% training from GPU contention.
  • Conditional empty-reset FK/observation: neutral in overlapped training and −1.7% in high-reset stepping; fully reverted.
  • Always-transposed response rows: faster physics, −2.7% training.
  • Early response-vector prefetch: +0.55% physics but −0.64% training from increased live-register pressure.
  • Drop tiled ABA generalized_rhs store: exact but about −0.6% physics; fully reverted.
  • Serial three-row contact scheduling: 1.06M versus 1.93M physics steps/s; row parallelism is essential.
  • Row blocks of 32 gain 0.56% isolated physics but lose 0.38% training. Blocks of 64 gain 0.65% G1 training overlap but lose 1.2–2.7% on contact-rich G1/H1/ANYmal fleets; retain the general 96-thread block.
  • Stride-3 contact prepare: policy exploits stale-contact physics and fails the true-physics gate.
  • Factor sub-warp tiling: +9% kernel time.
  • Compile-time generalized-contact mode specialization: exact focused tests pass, but static iteration expansion drops G1 physics about 3%; keep the compact runtime loop.
  • Fuse per-joint initialization into tiled factor: physically exact, but −2.3% G1 physics from increased kernel pressure.
  • Path-sparse Jacobian cache: −1.5% physics; indexed tile dots slowed every PGS update.
  • Remove ABA generalized-acceleration stores: neutral/slightly slower.
  • Full 96-row shared response cache: −14% from fixed loads and occupancy.
  • Adaptive two-link unit-wrench response basis: exact algebra, but −0.5–1.6% across G1, ANYmal, and H1 training/robot brackets.
  • No-inline ABA phase inside the contact megakernel: exact and graph-safe, but about −24% G1 training from device-call marshalling/local-stack traffic.
  • Launch bounds on the inline megakernel: four blocks leave its 122 registers unchanged; 20 blocks cap it to 96 with spills and regress training to about 834k/s.
  • Shared ABA-factor cache in contact-row blocks: mixed by articulation width/batch and −3–5% on smaller G1/ANYmal fleets; moving global reads to a synchronized shared phase is not a universal latency fix.
  • Row-builder local ancestor stack: −25%.
  • BF16 recurrent forward: +0.3%, removed.

Mission and non-negotiables

  • Main quest: maximize end-to-end PhoenX G1 RL training throughput while keeping repeatable successful training. Faster unsuccessful training does not count.
  • Optimize the reduced-coordinate path where training currently succeeds; improving maximal-coordinate training remains valuable, but do not abandon the proven walker.
  • Keep physics clean and physically grounded: conserve momentum, preserve reliable convergence, retain the hard-Hertz contact model where possible, and use no scene-specific hacks.
  • End-to-end CUDA graph capture is mandatory. Classic PhoenX must remain deterministic.
  • Never overfit to G1. Screen Anymal, H1/G1-class robots, contact-rich scenes, closed loops, mixed ordinary joints/constraints, multiple articulations per world, and both single- and multi-world execution.

How to continue

  • Be innovative. New algorithms and data layouts are welcome; the fastest work is work safely eliminated. Megakernels, Warp tile operations, compact shared memory, and reasonably sized wp.func_native helpers are allowed when evidence supports them.
  • Use Nsight Systems to rank end-to-end costs. Ask for Nsight Compute only when counters can change a decision; prepare one bounded script measuring one kernel, then place its single command in a prominent action box on this page.
  • Move quickly with focused mini-benchmarks and CUDA-graph regression tests. Run full from-scratch training and two robust gates only for meaningful survivors.
  • Compare against nanoG1, MJWarp, FeatherPGS, and full-coordinate PhoenX without trading away accuracy or reliability.
  • Commit measured gains when worthwhile. Keep this page compact, newest information first, remove stale entries, and open it in Chrome after every update.
  • Scope: prioritize PhoenX solver throughput; collision-pipeline gains are allowed when general and quality-preserving. The learner is currently out of scope. This page is the primary communication channel, including privileged-run requests in the top action box.
  • Immediate next step after the requested pause: return to the retained 923k/s branch. Avoid both rejected branches 67f286e6 and 51b834f4; the next candidate must delete a fleet-wide phase rather than add a complementary launch.
