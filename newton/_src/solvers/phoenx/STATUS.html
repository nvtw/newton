CORRECTED DIAGNOSIS1 Jul · latest

Maximal bilateral-joint enforcement is the remaining quality gap

The motor and training interfaces are now substantially ruled out. The evidence points to scalar maximal enforcement of the open G1 joint tree: exact articulated response improves learning, and letting that tree own its bilateral joints improves it again.

  • At the established 75M-sample comparison, reduced reaches about 0.93 tracking, hybrid about 0.76, and pure maximal about 0.60. This monotonic hierarchy is more informative than any single short run.
  • A new 64-world, two-second, graph-captured exploration diagnostic finishes in about five seconds. It uses identical smooth actions and reset noise, records first termination, and scores closure only while each world remains valid.
  • Pure maximal reaches up to 0.153 m locked helper-anchor error before termination at 10×8×1; reduced keeps the corresponding tree closure at numerical precision. More substeps reduce drift but do not restore reduced-like exploration survival.
  • One velocity-relax pass remains best. Zero or two do not improve first termination and are less robust after termination. Exact per-substep prepare and serial bidirectional joint ordering are no-ops for the relevant metrics.
  • Disabling armature diverges immediately, so reflected motor inertia is necessary. The physical parent-stator/child-rotor implementation remains covered by analytical, momentum, passivity, and live-update tests.
  • A direct quaternion/angular-row hinge formulation was derived and tested. It passes the two-body pendulum but improves G1 pre-terminal closure only marginally and worsens post-terminal robustness; it was rejected and fully reverted.
  • A matched 12.6M-sample control shows both reduced and hybrid are still in the standing phase: forward tracking 0.081 and 0.080. Short runs are therefore smoke gates, not quality verdicts; the long run is reserved for candidates that first improve deterministic mini-metrics.

Next solver candidate: an articulation-sized block solve of the maximal KKT bilateral rows, followed by ordinary deterministic PGS for contacts, limits, and loops. For an open tree, the linear-time factorization is mathematically equivalent to an articulated-body Schur solve; that equivalence is unavoidable, but body poses and velocities can remain the primary maximal state. This is a physical block preconditioner, not compliance, mass scaling, or relaxation.

Optimization ladder: algebraic and two-body invariants; five-second graph exploration/closure; open-loop nanoG1 trajectory parity; 24-iteration smoke; unchanged 75M walking gate; then Nsight end-to-end samples/s. Reduced is currently the successful oracle and is already at least as fast as maximal in matched G1 training, so improving its implementation remains the practical fallback.

MAXIMAL ROOT CAUSE1 Jul · latest

Motor physics is fixed; maximal tree conditioning is now the leading blocker

The maximal-coordinate G1 run is numerically stable and learns a smooth standing policy, but it still does not learn the frozen six-command walking gate. The latest evidence narrows this to schedule-sensitive whole-tree constraint response rather than a reward, action-mask, importer, or isolated motor bug.

  • The motor-armature defect is fixed physically: stator inertia is added to the parent and gear-reflected rotor inertia to the child. All focused CUDA-graph armature tests pass, including ankle-roll passivity, analytical periods, live updates, and momentum conservation.
  • The exact pinned nanoG1 source confirms that task V3 exposes 29 slots but deliberately masks actions 12–28. PhoenX controlling 12 leg actions is correct; expanding it to 29 would be a false fix.
  • At zero action, PhoenX and nanoG1 both report exactly four contacts per foot. Normal support is close at 329 versus 326 N and pose drift is small, but PhoenX produces about 71 versus 11 N tangential support. This contact mismatch is real.
  • However, reduced PhoenX shows a similar high tangential ratio and already trains successfully. Therefore contact parity should improve, but it cannot by itself explain why only maximal training fails.
  • A 100-control-step strong-action A/B is the clearest discriminator: maximal 5 substeps × 2 iterations diverges after 10 steps, while maximal 10×8 remains finite and approaches the reduced trajectory. Reduced is largely insensitive to 5×2 versus 10×8. Maximal PGS is therefore spending most of its work recovering tree coupling that reduced coordinates enforce exactly.
  • The corrected 10×8×1 maximal run reached 75.5M samples without numerical failure but settled into command-ignoring standing: full-gate performance 0.601 with 32 falls, fast-forward tracking 0.090, and failed backward/yaw batteries. More samples or reward deletion did not fix this basin.

What I am trying next: first add a graph-captured tree-response regression that measures one-frame maximal/reduced error across randomized G1 states and contacts. Then test the smallest physically grounded solver change: an exact articulation/tree projection for bilateral joint response once per temporal substep, with ordinary deterministic maximal PGS retained for contacts, loops, and other constraints. This is a Schur-complement preconditioner derived from the same constrained equations, not mass scaling or relaxation.

Decision: simply adding more scalar PGS iterations is unlikely to be the right solution. The clean options are (1) tree-projected maximal PGS, which preserves the general full-coordinate contact/loop interface while removing its worst conditioning, or (2) optimize the already successful reduced path. Reduced currently sustains about 241k samples/s versus roughly 225–250k/s for recent maximal runs, so it is already competitive with maximal rather than inherently slower. The main remaining speed gap is to nanoG1, not reduced versus maximal. I will keep pure maximal training as the acceptance target while using reduced as the correctness and throughput oracle.

MAXIMAL NOT PASSING1 Jul · latest

Pure full-coordinate G1 remains the acceptance target; a motor-path defect is now isolated

Pure articulation_mode="maximal" at 20 temporal substeps, 2 PGS iterations, and 1 velocity iteration is numerically stable with implicit constraint drives, but it has not learned the frozen six-command walking gate.

  • A 75.5M-sample run reached 0.575 battery tracking with 48 falls. Continuing the same policy to 151M samples changed this only to 0.577 with 31 falls. More training alone is therefore ruled out.
  • Removing PhoenX-only gait, swing-height, hip, and base-height shaping to reproduce nanoG1's published dense reward exactly also failed: 0.558 tracking and 78 falls at 75.5M samples. Reward-parity drift is not the primary blocker.
  • Frame-derived maximal joint velocities are much quieter and now have a CUDA-graph regression, but cleaner policy observations alone do not recover locomotion.
  • The remaining explicit-torque path is definitively broken in maximal mode: even a contact-free, zero-action G1 grows from 0.00044 rad/s to 5.25 million rad/s within ten 2ms substeps. This is independent of contacts.
  • A per-DoF passivity bisection isolates the failure to the two ankle-roll motors: 27/29 single-motor tests stay below 0.20 rad/s, while ankle roll reaches 556 and 1,163 rad/s in one frame.
  • The live reflected inertia is present, but child-only rotor inertia does not implement generalized armature on a free parent/child pair. The light ankle-pitch parent counter-rotates, leaving measured relative inverse inertia near 2,825 instead of the armature-conditioned value. The fix must represent M + JᵀAJ, including parent reaction, and preserve common rigid rotation.
  • A fixed-forward progress-reward control proves maximal PhoenX can move, improving forward error from 0.75 to 0.50, but the gait becomes fragile and falls. This connects the passivity defect to the standing local optimum without treating reward tuning as the fix.
  • Warm training throughput is about 232–234k samples/s, approximately 5.4× slower than nanoG1's pinned 1.276M/s. Performance optimization remains secondary until a maximal checkpoint passes.

Verdict: no, maximal G1 training does not work yet. Reduced/hybrid success does not satisfy the goal. Next is a physically correct fix for maximal generalized torque and velocity response, followed by the same graph-captured train-to-gate test.

ROOT CAUSE ISOLATED30 Jun

Pure-maximal G1 armature is not yet reliable

A controlled free-space, zero-action sweep isolates the failure to the maximal-coordinate armature path rather than contacts, friction, policy actions, or an insufficient iteration count.

  • With armature disabled, the full 29-joint G1 remains at its home pose to numerical precision over the test horizon.
  • With physical armature enabled, the same chain diverges. Raising the schedule as far as 10 substeps × 64 PGS iterations does not fix it and convergence is non-monotonic.
  • The current pendulum and three-link armature tests are therefore insufficient. The fix must add a deep spatial-chain, CUDA-graph regression and preserve momentum and the exact configured armature.
  • The equal-work substep-versus-iteration claim cannot be judged honestly until this defect is removed.
  • An exact local 2×2 drive/armature block did not improve the failure, proving the problematic coupling spans the tree joint-lock rows rather than just the motor row.
  • Armature-row relaxation at 0.1 passes the G1 stability check but destroys physical accuracy: pendulum period errors reach 67% and three-link modal errors reach 47%. It is rejected, not retained.
  • The clean next implementation is an exact tree-coupled maximal mass operator for M_body + Jᵀ A J (tree LDL/Woodbury), while keeping maximal joint and contact rows. This has the correct fixed-time-step physics and avoids tuning parameters.

Existing Nsight traces also identify the first performance targets. In classic contact-rich G1, radix sorting consumes 21.1% of GPU kernel time, block-world prepare/iterate 20.8%, greedy coloring 8.0%, and scans 5.9%. In the articulated contact-rich path, generalized contact construction/solve/gather/finalize dominates roughly 55% before factorization and momentum passes. Correctness remains first; these measurements define the subsequent optimization work.

HYBRID EXPLAINED30 Jun · 22:08

Is hybrid the perfect full/reduced-coordinate solution?

No, but it is a clean and useful middle ground. Hybrid keeps every full-coordinate PhoenX joint, loop, limit, motor, and contact row in deterministic PGS. Instead of applying each row through independent rigid-body inverse masses, it applies impulses through an exact linear-time articulated-body response for each tree.

  • What it gains: exact tree inertia coupling and armature response, much better conditioning for stiff humanoid drives, momentum conservation, and reduced-like robot stability while retaining maximal PGS constraints and contacts.
  • What remains maximal: loop closures, contacts, friction, limits, and joint-row residuals are still iterated by PGS. Their convergence and accuracy are not automatically identical to a direct reduced-coordinate solve.
  • Applicability: it supports multiple articulations per world and many worlds, plus ordinary contacts and maximal constraints. The accelerated path currently requires standard PhoenX, the fast-tail schedule, and no mass splitting; unsupported scene features need broader engineering.
  • Performance: it is not free. Hybrid evaluates articulated dynamics while also retaining maximal rows, so it cannot honestly be called full speed of the pure full-coordinate solver. Earlier matched measurements showed meaningful overhead; Nsight profiling is still pending.
  • Verdict: excellent for stiff tree robots embedded in contact-rich maximal scenes, but not yet a universal final solver. A world-class endpoint would preserve its conditioning while eliminating redundant work and proving quality/performance across loops, many articulations, dense contacts, and diverse robots.
