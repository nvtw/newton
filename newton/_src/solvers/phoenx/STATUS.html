THROUGHPUT PARITY · QUALITY PENDINGLatest matched G1 evidence

Compact projected full coordinates erase the reduced-coordinate throughput deficit

The exact projector now stores body mass, symmetric world inertia, and joint shift in 10 scalars per body instead of loading two full spatial matrices (72 scalars). It reconstructs the identical block-diagonal spatial mass and shift transform at use sites. At 8,192 worlds with the same 5-substep, 2-PGS-iteration graph-leapfrog schedule, fresh matched runs place projected maximal and reduced coordinates at approximately 649k samples/s each; two preceding projected repeats measured 651–653k/s. The honest result is parity within run noise, after an 8.3% projected deficit before compaction.

  • Physical stability is promising: a 75-iteration projected G1 run and its frozen held-out screen produced zero falls, while graph tests preserve closures, momentum, contacts, and bitwise determinism.
  • Policy quality is not yet sufficient: seed 42 reached held-out battery performance 0.643 versus the frozen 0.680 early gate; the retained reduced-coordinate seed-42 result at the same iteration is 0.708.
  • Reducing projected maximal from five to four or three substeps is not viable: long-horizon random-action tests terminate earlier and constraint error rises sharply.
  • Cross-mode checkpoint swaps confirm materially different coupled dynamics, while contact-free and grounded open-loop trajectories make projected maximal at least as close to the nanoG1 reference as reduced coordinates. There is no evidence of a basic projector correctness bug.
  • The compact representation plus four independent articulation warps per CUDA block improves the isolated graph-captured physics bracket from 920k to 1.050M environment steps/s (+14.1%). The ordering and equations are unchanged.
  • Ten focused CUDA-graph gates pass after compaction: floating momentum, floating and contacting loops, mixed trees, D6, multiple articulations per world, bitwise determinism, and the G1 joint step response.
  • A production-scale Nsight trace of the former representation identified the exact projector recurrence as 36.9% of measured GPU kernel duration; gather and publish added 12.8%. The counter capture showed zero spilling, 33.3% register-limited occupancy, 72.7% L2 throughput, 48.6% LG-memory throttle, and only 4.45 active threads per warp.
  • Register/shuffle, one-thread-per-articulation, and four-articulations-per-warp sub-warp prototypes were measured and rejected: they were respectively unboundedly slow, 38% slower, and 7.7% slower. The retained layout preserves one warp per articulation and merely packs four independent warps into a block.
  • Promotion is still blocked by policy quality, not throughput: the existing projected seed-42 iteration-75 screen scores 0.643 versus the frozen 0.680 gate and the matched reduced result of 0.708. A bounded retraining race is required before this affects time-to-policy.

NEW AUTHORITATIVE METRIC4 Jul

Cold-start wall-clock time to a qualified policy

The clock starts immediately before launching the training process with no policy, replay, optimizer, normalization, or curriculum state. It stops at the first held-out evaluation that satisfies the task gate. Environment/model creation, normal Warp cache loading, graph capture, rollout, learning, evaluation, checkpointing, and any population overhead are included.

  • Primary deployment clock: a normal ready installation with its persistent Warp kernel cache in the declared state. A cleared-JIT-cache first-install clock is reported separately so compiler noise cannot hide training improvements.
  • Success: task-specific thresholds are frozen before an optimization run, evaluated on held-out randomized commands/initial states, and must pass in consecutive evaluation windows. Failed runs are not dropped.
  • Expectation value: time-to-policy is a random variable. The primary estimate is expected wall-clock cost to success under a frozen restart policy: mean successful-run time plus mean charged failure time × (1−p)/p, where p is success probability. A predeclared early rejection is charged its observed time; crashes, timeouts, and ordinary misses are conservatively charged the full cutoff H. Also report uncertainty and raw seed outcomes.
  • Censoring: timed-out and failed runs are retained. They are not assigned the mean of successful runs; the restart-cost estimate explicitly penalizes unreliability. A configuration with no successes by H is unresolved/dominated, not silently excluded.
  • Sequential judgment: one run may reject only a correctness failure or overwhelming regression. Ordinary candidates get paired seeds and adaptive racing; uncertain results receive more runs. Finalists use enough independent seeds to estimate expected cost and uncertainty.
  • Seed protocol: vary seeds for policy initialization, environment/reset randomization, command sampling, and learner ordering. Compare candidates on the same paired development seed bank, then confirm on a separate unseen seed bank. Because PhoenX/PhoenX-RL is deterministic per seed, every trial is exactly reproducible and seed effects are not confused with runtime noise.
  • Evaluation budget: frequent cheap screening evaluations trigger the full held-out gate only when promising. Both screening and confirmation time count, and stopping rules are frozen to avoid cherry-picking.
  • Generalization: development uses a rotating subset of the eight task families; promotion requires locomotion diversity and a held-out task. No robot-name solver specializations.
  • Allowed levers: timestep/substeps, full or reduced coordinates, contact/solver schedules, PPO variants, curricula, recurrent/feed-forward policies, mixed precision, PBT, and overlap. All consumed wall time and GPU work count.
  • Hard gates: physically defensible simulation, stable contacts/joints, no reward exploitation of stale or degraded physics, CUDA graph support for measured paths, and versatile reusable RL code.
  • Implemented: the G1 runner emits raw per-seed outcomes and a continuously updated suite record. It reports empirical success probability, Wilson uncertainty, successful-run time, full-cutoff restart charges, expected attempts, and bootstrapped expected time to success.
  • Next: freeze compact gates for a quadruped and a structurally different task, then establish paired-seed cold-start baselines before changing solver or RL recipes.

INTEGRATED CANDIDATE · GRAPH CAPTURED9 Jul

Projected maximal robots are stable and faster than reduced PhoenX

The additive articulation_mode="maximal_projected" path now has two exact GPU projectors: a branch-light free-root revolute specialization and a fused general kernel for anchored or floating trees mixing fixed, revolute, prismatic, ball, and reducible D6/universal joints. Existing explicit "hybrid" behavior is unchanged. Remaining unsupported trees automatically use pure reduced ownership, avoiding the old hybrid double solve.

  • The recurrence still matches dense KKT projection across 1,000 randomized anchored/floating trees with 1–32 bodies, mixed fixed/revolute/prismatic/ball subspaces, and six mass/inertia decades. Maximum dense error is 2.3×10−7, allowed-coordinate impulse is 3.4×10−7, and kinetic energy never increases.
  • Matched graph-captured G1 at 5 substeps × 2 PGS iterations keeps all 32 seeded worlds alive for 50 aggressive-action steps with 8.6×10−5 m peak / 1.5×10−6 m RMS anchor error. Ordinary maximal fails almost immediately in the same screen.
  • The latest intentional-readback bracket measures 21.79k control-steps/s for projected maximal, 12.03k for reduced, and 5.46k for established hybrid: projected maximal is 1.81× reduced and 3.99× old hybrid in this diagnostic. This is not yet an end-to-end training-throughput or time-to-policy claim.
  • Production CUDA-graph tests now cover anchored and floating mixed trees, reducible D6 universal joints, generic-D6 pure-reduced fallback, multiple articulations per world, bitwise determinism, floating momentum, recovered-reaction orthogonality, loop-joint exclusion, active rigid contacts, and repeated graph replay.
  • The integration exposed a general maximal-coordinate reset bug: importing a rotated state left world inverse inertia at its previous orientation for one frame. Rebuilding it at the import boundary reduces repeated floating-tree angular-momentum error from about 9.3×10−3 to 8×10−6. Focused momentum, energy, armature, and live-armature-update modules pass.
  • ANYmal and H1 scratch gates previously showed the same robot-independent closure improvement. Permanent cross-robot tests and policy training are still required before this path can affect the primary time-to-policy score.
  • The general gather/tree/publish phases are fused into one graph node per projection pass. In a 512-world mixed-tree bracket with 6,656 active contacts, projected maximal measures 338 µs/step versus 581 µs reduced and 685 µs old hybrid: 1.72× and 2.03× faster respectively. Joint-only tiny trees still favor reduced ownership, so this is a regime result rather than a universal ranking.
  • Remaining limitation: compliant cable behavior, distance inequalities, and generic non-reducible D6 trees are not rigidified by the projector; they use pure reduced ownership. The explicit old hybrid path remains available for comparison but is no longer the automatic fallback.
  • Contacts, loop closures, limits, and other ordinary constraints remain in deterministic PhoenX GS; contact topology is never cached across collision frames. A graph-captured contacting four-bar and a floating internal loop both remain closed, and the mixed path is bitwise deterministic. Long-horizon contact-rich robot training and held-out time-to-policy remain promotion gates.

Retained derivation and promotion gates: MAXIMAL_JOINT_TREE_PROJECTOR.md. Reproducible ideal kernel: bench_maximal_joint_tree_projector.py. No user profiling action is currently required.

PROMISING PROTOTYPE · NOT PRODUCTION9 Jul

Exact target-wrench support solve closes the 400:1 stack gap

The support-tree experiment now runs against real PhoenX contact rows after ordinary bias-free Gauss–Seidel relaxation. It computes the complete target patch wrench, redistributes that wrench to the actual contact points, and commits the correction only when every reconstructed point force lies inside its Coulomb cone. Production PhoenX remains unchanged while impact and topology-change gates are completed.

  • At the difficult 5-substep × 2-PGS schedule, the actual 400:1 two-box stack now has zero measured residual body velocity and relative mean top/ground load errors of 1.5×10−7 / 5.9×10−8. The preceding delta-only distribution left about 7% load error because an arbitrary warm-start distribution could make an otherwise feasible correction appear infeasible.
  • A 100 N lateral load is accepted for every sampled frame and remains static. Under 5000 N, the upper patch fails the cone gate and slides/separates; only the independently grounded lower component remains eligible. This is the required physical behavior, not an unconditional freeze.
  • The solve remains a preconditioned colored-GS path: ordinary PhoenX PGS establishes the nonlinear contact state, the exact tree correction handles a coupled sticking mode, and any rank, topology, normal-alignment, or friction failure leaves the ordinary GS result untouched.
  • A graph-captured dynamic prototype builds deterministic parent/level/component data and solves 1024 branched forests of 32 bodies in about 20.6 µs on RTX PRO 6000 Blackwell. The earlier solve-only result is 10.3 µs for 8192 five-body trees.
  • Contact connectivity is transient and will be rebuilt from the current collision result. Joint/body incidence is persistent and may be preprocessed once, but no contact parentage will be cached across collision frames. Multiple contact components are accepted or rejected independently.
  • Still required: drop/impact and restitution behavior, opening/sliding contacts, changing manifolds, momentum/energy accounting, non-tree fallback, joints mixed with contacts, deterministic single- and multi-world execution, and end-to-end CUDA graph tests. No production integration will land before these gates pass.

The retained derivation and idealized GPU oracle are in docs/SUPPORT_TREE_CONTACT_PRECONDITIONER.md and benchmarks/experimental/bench_support_tree_patch.py. The real-PhoenX integration remains an isolated scratch experiment.

ACTIVE · MAXIMAL GS CONVERGENCE9 Jul

A steady-window benchmark exposes the 100:1 propagation gap

The new graph-captured heavy-on-light benchmark averages contact load over 120 settled frames and records RMS body speed, separation, and lateral drift. This avoids accepting a lucky instantaneous impulse sample from an oscillatory solve.

  • At the G1-like 5 substeps × 2 PGS iterations, the heavy box falls from the stack and its supported-load error is 73.9%. At 5×4 the stack remains intact, but mean load error is still 45.8%.
  • 8×4 reduces mean load error to 25.2%; 8×8 reaches 3.3%, with 0.025 / 0.034 m/s bottom/top RMS speed and only 0.020 m maximum lateral drift.
  • At fixed five substeps, 5×12 reaches 2.7% error for 100:1 and remains 2.8× faster than reduced 5×2 on the matched 512-world G1 physics diagnostic. At 400:1 it still has 14.9% error, confirming the benchmark scales beyond one tuned ratio.
  • The old 40×32 unit-test schedule is therefore far above the current convergence frontier, but ordinary RL-like budgets remain insufficient. Increasing substeps alone does not replace GS propagation in this case.
  • Split SOR at 1.2 reduced drift and some instantaneous residuals but did not materially improve the time-averaged load; it is rejected as a standalone fix.
  • A frozen seed-42 maximal 5×12 G1 screen still fails decisively: 488k samples/s, but battery performance is only 0.154 with 286/600 falls. Contact load convergence is therefore not the root cause of maximal G1 learning failure; joint-chain/control dynamics remain distinct work.
  • The next solver experiment keeps deterministic colored Gauss–Seidel: use a support-aware preconditioner or targeted physical GS sweeps for difficult rows, then finish with ordinary unscaled sweeps. Momentum, energy, load transfer, arbitrary contact counts, and graph capture remain hard gates.

RETAINED CANDIDATE · CONTACT ROW LAYOUT9 Jul

Color-ordered contact rows move fixed Kapla to 74.9 FPS

The completed source-counter pass confirms that the dominant rigid-contact iterate is a gather-latency problem: 78.2% of issue spacing is long-scoreboard stall and Nsight reports 1.81 million excess global sectors—78% of all sectors. Arithmetic, DRAM saturation, __ldg(), and sleeping are not the target.

  • The safe opt-in path keeps canonical rows for ingest, warm start, and prepare. After prepare, one thread per contact gathers only the 24 rows consumed by iterate/relax into color order; after relax, one thread per contact scatters only the three accumulated impulses back.
  • An exclusive device scan builds offsets and a packed-to-canonical map once per rebuilt contact graph. There is no hard-coded contact count, binary search, or linked-list traversal. A CUDA-graph unit test covers interleaved 1-, 7-, and 13-contact columns.

  • The 2×2 scene with 45,360 bricks improves 23.87 → 28.29 FPS in the matched run (+18.5%), so the gain grows with contact load rather than overfitting to 1×1. Drift, maximum displacement, and height match the paired baseline.
  • The post-change Systems trace measures the persistent iterate at 5.59 µs average, down from 6.81 µs at the previous checkpoint. Contact-thread gather costs 12.26 µs per substep and only 1.1% of kernel time; iterate falls from 51.4% to 46.9% of the profile.
  • The real-data Kapla settle test and launch-shape suite pass under CUDA graph capture. The arbitrary-count row round-trip test also passes.
  • A smaller response-cache experiment was rejected: it helped 1×1 by 1.4% but hurt 2×2 by 1.4%. The earlier column-thread row gather was also rejected after regressing 2×2; contact-thread staging is the version that scales.

Cold-start G1 trials are executable and failure-aware

bench_time_to_policy launches each G1 attempt in a fresh Python process, so imports, CUDA initialization, normal cache loading, graph capture, training, checkpoint reload, and held-out evaluation all contribute to wall time. Candidate recipes cannot improve the score merely by failing more often.

  • Training seeds and held-out gate seeds are recorded separately and paired in a frozen order. Candidate comparisons can reuse the same banks; final confirmation can use an unseen bank.
  • Qualification can require multiple successive held-out windows. A failure resets the streak, and the child stops only when the configured streak is complete.
  • Timeouts, crashes, and ordinary quality misses are retained as censored trials and charged the full restart horizon. A child-declared predeclared early rejection is charged its observed wall time. Zero observed successes produces an explicit infinite-cost state, serialized as strict JSON null plus a flag.
  • Twenty focused protocol, optimizer, determinism, and CUDA-graph tests pass. A forced 10 ms timeout is correctly censored and charged.
  • The smoke's 3.93 s value used trivial two-world/two-iteration gates and is only protocol validation—not a training result. No new performance claim is made yet.
  • ANYmal's existing pure-PhoenX forward curriculum is now a second task adapter. Its final no-reset walking gate is repeated with distinct held-out disturbance seeds, so confirmation windows exercise different physical trajectories.
  • A tiny ANYmal graph-leapfrog smoke trained, checkpointed, reloaded, evaluated, correctly missed the walking gate, and was retained as a 180 s charged failure. This validates failure accounting; it is not a learning result.
RETAINED · CLOSED LINKAGE TASK4 Jul

DR Legs provides structurally different coverage

  • DR Legs hold adds a structurally different maximal-coordinate closed linkage. Its no-reset gate applies seeded whole-robot horizontal velocity impulses and independently checks falls, survival, pose success, pelvis height/uprightness/drift, finite state, and closed-loop anchor residual.
  • The untrained DR policy is correctly rejected: all 64 kicked worlds fall, with about 12–13% survival. Anchor residual remains below 0.5 mm, separating policy failure from a broken linkage solve.
  • After 50 production-size PPO updates, survival improves to 15.5% and minimum pelvis height to 0.164 m, but the policy still fails. This is useful direction, not a passing result; no DR time-to-policy baseline is claimed yet.
  • A large-world ordering bug was isolated: building the evaluation model after capturing but before first launching the training graph triggered CUDA error 700. The logically correct order—zero-training gate first, then capture—removes the failure at 2048 and 4096 training worlds.

MEASURED · BUILDER WIN REJECTED END TO END3 Jul

Faster row construction alone does not move training throughput

The requested post-cache Nsight Compute capture and two exact follow-up prototypes now isolate the remaining problem. The retained headline stays at 923k samples/s; no higher number is being claimed.

  • After the retained shared-factor cache, contact-row construction falls 943.6 → 879.7 µs (−6.8%), but remains latency-bound: about 68% long-scoreboard issue spacing, only 19.8% scheduler cycles with an eligible warp, and 79% excessive global sectors.
  • An exact compressed 12D body-mobility cache improves its isolated response calculation by 6.7%, but only about 486–503 of 8192 training worlds are eligible. Build and launch overhead lowers complete training throughput, so the path is rejected.
  • An exact ≤4-contact shared-row builder reproduces Jacobians and articulated responses bit-for-bit and improves stable isolated builder medians about 8.5% (222 → 203 µs).
  • Integrated training nevertheless reaches 892.4k/s versus an immediate clean retained control at 922.8k/s: a real 3.3% regression. The extra launch and global row publication erase the local saving; this implementation is rejected.
  • Direct shared-memory PGS variants were also exact, but register/instruction pressure made them 5–40% slower than the proven tile solve.
  • Compiler-supported noinline phase bodies also fail: the exact by-value call ABI reaches about 793k/s, while passing aggregate descriptors by reference falls to about 509k/s because array/member accesses stay behind pointer loads. Both forms are rejected.
  • An exact contact-space Delassus solve and combined articulated impulse application pass graph parity, but reach only 892k/s after depth parallelization; they reduce storage without reducing the dominant per-row articulated work.
  • The next credible large experiment must remove row publication and phase boundaries while also reducing—not repackaging—the O(contact rows × articulation size) response construction.
RETAINED · CROSS-SCENE ROW CACHE3 Jul

Shared articulation factors remove the row builder's worst latency

The existing contact-row Nsight report showed a 943.6 µs kernel with 76.7% of issue spacing in long-scoreboard stalls and 73% excessive global sectors. Every one of 96 row threads repeatedly loaded the same motion subspaces, articulated-inertia columns, and inverse joint factors.

  • The row block now cooperatively loads those exact factors into about 4.6 KB of shared memory once, then evaluates the unchanged articulated response equations and deterministic row order.
  • Matched G1 graph-leapfrog training improves 913.8k → 923.0k samples/s (+1.0%). The retained nanoG1 gap is now about 1.38×.
  • Cross-scene gates also improve: 512-world ANYmal +0.5%, H1 +1.7%; 64 articulations in one world ANYmal +1.5%, H1 +1.7%. Final state magnitudes are identical.
  • Six focused CUDA regressions pass: exact generalized rows, arbitrary contact pages, persistent/reference parity, multiple grounded articulations per world, deterministic dense contacts, and spatial momentum conservation.
  • Commit 2ce2656a is retained on twidmer/experiment-phoenx-persistent-articulations. Repository-wide pre-commit remains blocked only by unrelated pre-existing lint/typo failures; focused lint and formatting pass.

Next decisions

Build the measurement loop first

Optimizing throughput before measuring first-passing wall time can reward the wrong solver, timestep, curriculum, or learner. The benchmark must make failures and evaluation cost visible and produce one comparable JSON record per run.

First baseline set: G1 plus a quadruped and a structurally different task, using current proven recipes. Then compare safe timestep/schedule changes, reduced versus full coordinates where valid, and RL changes by time-to-gate—not isolated SPS.

Do not repeat

  • Separate 32-row resident Jacobian/response solve: bit-exact but −8.6% G1 training from a second launch, fixed shared loads, and occupancy.
  • One-pass ABA / pre-contact-only Coriolis: +6% physics but severe momentum drift.
  • Compile-time velocity-only publish kernel: exact but +0.1% (neutral).
  • Factor/contact multi-stream overlap: −2.7% training from GPU contention.
  • Conditional empty-reset FK/observation: neutral in overlapped training and −1.7% in high-reset stepping; fully reverted.
  • Always-transposed response rows: faster physics, −2.7% training.
  • Early response-vector prefetch: +0.55% physics but −0.64% training from increased live-register pressure.
  • Drop tiled ABA generalized_rhs store: exact but about −0.6% physics; fully reverted.
  • Serial three-row contact scheduling: 1.06M versus 1.93M physics steps/s; row parallelism is essential.
  • Row blocks of 32 gain 0.56% isolated physics but lose 0.38% training. Blocks of 64 gain 0.65% G1 training overlap but lose 1.2–2.7% on contact-rich G1/H1/ANYmal fleets; retain the general 96-thread block.
  • Stride-3 contact prepare: policy exploits stale-contact physics and fails the true-physics gate.
  • Factor sub-warp tiling: +9% kernel time.
  • Compile-time generalized-contact mode specialization: exact focused tests pass, but static iteration expansion drops G1 physics about 3%; keep the compact runtime loop.
  • Fuse per-joint initialization into tiled factor: physically exact, but −2.3% G1 physics from increased kernel pressure.
  • Path-sparse Jacobian cache: −1.5% physics; indexed tile dots slowed every PGS update.
  • Remove ABA generalized-acceleration stores: neutral/slightly slower.
  • Full 96-row shared response cache: −14% from fixed loads and occupancy.
  • Adaptive two-link unit-wrench response basis: exact algebra, but −0.5–1.6% across G1, ANYmal, and H1 training/robot brackets.
  • No-inline ABA phase inside the contact megakernel: exact and graph-safe, but about −24% G1 training from device-call marshalling/local-stack traffic.
  • Launch bounds on the inline megakernel: four blocks leave its 122 registers unchanged; 20 blocks cap it to 96 with spills and regress training to about 834k/s.
  • Shared ABA-factor cache in contact-row blocks: mixed by articulation width/batch and −3–5% on smaller G1/ANYmal fleets; moving global reads to a synchronized shared phase is not a universal latency fix.
  • Row-builder local ancestor stack: −25%.
  • BF16 recurrent forward: +0.3%, removed.
AI agent handoff

Mission and non-negotiables

  • Main quest: minimize expected cold-start wall-clock cost to a passing policy with PhoenX + PhoenX RL under a frozen restart policy.
  • Count the whole process and every population/evaluation cost. Estimate a distribution over varied, logged seeds—not one lucky runtime—and retain failed/censored seeds.
  • Keep physics clean and physically grounded. Larger timesteps and full coordinates are welcome only when policy and physical-quality gates pass.
  • Do not overfit G1. Use the pure-PhoenX Ant, Humanoid, Go2, G1, H1, ANYmal-C, and DR Legs tasks as a rotating development/held-out suite.
  • Measured solver/RL paths must retain CUDA graph support. Reusable RL improvements must not be task-specific hacks.

How to continue

  • First implement the unified time-to-policy protocol and establish honest baselines; do not infer convergence time from SPS or sample count alone.
  • Use paired seeds, cheap learning-curve checkpoints, and sequential racing for iteration. Do not reject an ordinary candidate from one bad run; spend additional seeds when the expected-cost comparison remains uncertain.
  • Optimize solver, collision, environment, policy, learner, curriculum, precision, and overlap jointly. PBT is allowed, but its full wall time counts.
  • Track supporting diagnostics: paired development seed, held-out evaluation seed bank, samples/s, physics steps/s, sample count to gate, evaluation quality, timestep, GPU memory, and compilation/setup time.
  • Keep this page compact and newest-first, update it whenever the decision state changes, and open it in Chrome after every update.
  • Immediate next step: checkpoint the compact exact projected-maximal path, then prototype deterministic parallel tree contraction as a maximal-coordinate joint preconditioner. Test high mass ratios, mixed joints, contacts, loops, G1, ANYmal, and H1 before promotion.
